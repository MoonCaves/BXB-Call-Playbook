Internal · The Closer's Playbook

The Closing Call

Indoctrination arc with diagnostic checkpoints. Roughly 45–60 min.
Before you dial

Pull what you already know.

Wherever the prospect came from — book interview, Saturday circle, referral, or a cold conversation — there's almost always something on the record. Pull these five things into a single line of notes:

  • The Biz. What he sells, who he sells to, revenue range, whether he's the one selling or has a team.
  • The Open. What he said about why he was open to this conversation. Use his exact words. That's the wedge.
  • The Slowdown. The biggest thing in his way right now. This is where the Lone Wolf is hiding.
  • The Bar. What he said would need to be true for this to be worth it. You'll hand this back to him at the close.
  • The Prior. Mastermind, coach, peer board, retreats, online community, or nothing. Tells you which objections will land hot.

If the prospect doesn't yet have a working idea of what BXB is, send the offer doc and a Loom walkthrough before this call. This script assumes a baseline.

What he's compared this to

Prior experience changes the map.

Whatever he's been in before is the lens he'll use to evaluate BXB. Knowing this in advance tells you which beats need extra weight and which objections are loaded.

PriorWhat he'll be looking for / wary of
Mastermind Wary of guru-and-audience format. Wants peers, not a stage. Lean into the contribution model and the rotating Pack.
1:1 coach Loyal to the coach. Position BXB as the missing peer layer, not a replacement. Don't ask him to choose.
Men's group / circle Already values the work. Sell the business and contribution layer — that's what's been missing for him.
Peer board (YPO, EO, Vistage, TIGER21) Knows the value of peers. Wary of paying again. Lean into the inner work, the in-person, and the BXB operating system — none of those rooms have it.
Online community / cohort program Burned out on Slack and Zoom-only. Lean hard into in-person, two events a week, the calendar.
Retreats / events Knows the high. Knows the come-down. Lean into continuity — Pack, weekly calls, the year-round container.
Digital course / cohort Has unfinished programs in his Drive. Wary of another thing he won't show up for. The conditional guarantee is built for him.
Nothing — first time No reference points. Move slowly. Spend more on the why before the what. The 60-day arc will be the most important thing he hears.
I.

Re-anchor

2 min
BEAT 01
Land the frame
≈ 2 min
This is a continuation, not a sales call.

Your job

  • Open warm but not casual. This is not small talk.
  • Reflect back two or three specific things from what you already know about him. By name. By detail.
  • Set the frame: this is about whether the room is a fit for him and he's a fit for the room. Both directions.

Language to pull from

"From what you and I have already talked about / from what I picked up at the circle / from what [referrer] told me — a few things stood out: [thing one], [thing two]. I want to go deeper on those, and push back on me if I get any of it wrong."
"This isn't a pitch call. By the end of this I'll have a sense of whether the men in the room would want to go to war with you. And you'll know whether this is the kind of room you've actually been looking for. Fair?"
Don't re-ask things you already know. If you re-ask, you signal you weren't paying attention the first time.
II.

The real question

5 min
BEAT 02
Get past the business stuff into the why-now
≈ 5 min
Shut up. Let the silence do the work.

The opening question — pick the one that fits him

"When you pictured your life at this revenue level — back when you were starting out — what did you think would be different by the time you got here?"
"You've got the freedom most guys are still chasing. What's the actual day-to-day in Bali like for you right now?"
"What's the next chapter look like, if you got really honest with yourself?"

Follow-ups, only if the silence breaks before he does

  • "What's actually different now versus what you imagined?"
  • "Who do you talk to about the real stuff? Not business — the actual stuff."
  • "When was the last time you had a conversation that actually moved something for you?"

What he'll actually say

Things that come out of his mouth
  • "Honestly, I don't know what's next."
  • "Business is going fine, I just feel a bit stuck."
  • "Bali's wild — but most of my friends here are pretty surface."
  • "My old crew back home is on a different trajectory now."
  • "I've been here two years and I've made maybe two real friends."
  • "I'm doing well but it doesn't feel like I thought it would."
  • "I'm bored, kind of."
  • "I want to build something bigger but I don't know what."
  • "Everyone here's either just travelling through or selling something."
What's actually underneath
  • The Lone Wolf identity is exhausted but still running the show.
  • The lifestyle goal got delivered and it didn't feel like the prize.
  • Friendships in Bali reset every 3–6 months — no continuity.
  • No room to talk honestly about the next chapter, or about what's not working.
  • Quiet drift. Comfort with no compass.
  • He's outgrown the version of himself that built the business.
Don't rescue him from the silence. Don't pivot to BXB. Don't solve. Mirror what he said in his own words — slightly compressed — and wait.
III.

Name the Lone Wolf

10 min
First indoctrination beat. You're not explaining the Lone Wolf — you're diagnosing it using the words he just gave you.
BEAT 03
Diagnose, don't describe
≈ 10 min

The frame — adapt to his actual language

"What you just described has a name. We call it the Lone Wolf. Most of the men I meet at your level have done well alone — built the business themselves, climbed it themselves. On paper it works. The revenue's there, the freedom's there, the control's there."
"The thing nobody warned them about: at some point being the man who can do it all alone stops being the win. You said [his exact phrase — 'I'm doing well but it doesn't feel like I thought it would' / 'I don't really know what's next' / 'most of my friends here are pretty surface']. That's not a mood. That's the ceiling."
"The Lone Wolf isn't a flaw. It's an operating system. It got you here. It eliminated excuses, forced you to learn fast, made you self-reliant in a way most men never get. But it was never meant to be permanent. It's stage one. And if you stay in stage one long enough, the same skill that built the business becomes the thing that's keeping you small."

Tie it back to him — specifically

  • Use his exact words, not yours. "When you said [X] — that's the wolf talking."
  • Don't generalise. Point.
  • If he laughs nervously or jokes, hold the frame. Don't joke back.

What you're looking for

  • The hard nod or the long silence. He's been waiting for someone to name this. Move forward.
  • Push-back. "I don't think that's me." Don't fight. Ask: "What would you call it?" Let him define it. The wolf will surface again later if it's real.
  • Deflection or jokes. He's uncomfortable. Good sign. Hold the frame. Don't laugh it off with him.
Don't name it before you've heard enough. Diagnose too early and it's a cold read. Diagnose after he's spoken and it's a mirror.
IV.

The paradox

10 min
If beat 3 landed, he knows he's in stage one. This is where you tell him the hard part: the very thing that built him is the thing keeping him stuck.
BEAT 04
The skill that built you is the skill blocking you
≈ 10 min

Set up the contrast — Sovereignty as stage two

"Stage two has a name too. We call it Sovereignty. And it's not what most guys think. It's not independence taken further. It's not self-sufficiency on steroids — that's just a louder version of the wolf. Sovereignty is the inner mastery of your own mind, values, and direction. The internal foundation that makes everything external — the money, the freedom, the lifestyle — finally feel like it means something."
"Without it, freedom turns into drift. The money's there but you can't tell what it's for anymore. With it, every part of your life starts to compound."

Then the paradox — this is the wedge

"Here's the part nobody tells you. The skill that built your business is the exact skill that's now keeping you stuck. You got here by being the guy who didn't need anyone. That worked. It built the thing. But you can't out-Lone-Wolf your way out of being a Lone Wolf."
"Sovereignty doesn't get built alone. The men who try end up isolated, optimising in circles, winning on paper and losing on the inside. It gets built through brotherhood — not because brotherhood is some soft, nice-to-have thing, but because it's the actual mechanism. Men become Sovereign by being seen, challenged, and held by other men on the same path. There's no other way."
"The thing you're going to have to give up to get the next chapter is the identity that built this one. That's the trade. Most guys don't make it. The ones who do — they end up running rooms like the one we built."

Why this works

  • You've named the thing he's most proud of (his self-reliance) as the thing that's costing him.
  • You've reframed brotherhood from "soft" to "engineering". It's not feelings — it's structural.
  • You've put a price on the next chapter — and the price is identity, not money. That makes the actual money feel small in comparison.
Don't rush into BXB here. Let the paradox sit for a full beat before the vehicle shows up.
V.

Checkpoint

3 min · do not skip
Hand the frame back to him.
"Does any of this land, or am I off?"

You've just done 25 minutes of philosophy. He needs a chance to agree, push back, or redirect. If you skip this and roll into the pitch, you've talked at him. If you ask this, you've talked with him — and his answer tells you exactly how to close.

If he leans in
"Yeah, this is me." / "You just described my life." / "I've felt this for a while." → You've earned the right to present the room. Go to Beat 6.
If he hesitates
"Partly." / "Some of it." → Ask: "What part's off?" Adjust. Don't move on until he's with you. Then Beat 6.
If he pushes back
"Not really — I've got good people around me." → Don't argue. Ask one of these instead: "What would your last day look like, if you nailed it?" · "Who's going to be in the room when you turn sixty?" · "Whose chapter are you in, beyond your own family's?" Let him answer. You may not close today. That's fine.
If he goes silent
Wait. Do not fill the silence. He's processing. Thirty seconds of quiet here is worth more than ten minutes of talk.
This beat is not a formality. It is the point on the call where the prospect actually decides — not when you ask for the close. Ask the question. Then stop talking until he answers.
VI.

The room

10 min
Present BXB only as the vehicle for what he just agreed is true. Not a feature list — the answer to his stated problem.
BEAT 06
BXB as the vehicle, not the product
≈ 10 min

The opener — anchor to the mother brand first

"So here's what we built, and why I think it matters for you specifically. The mother brand is Bali Brotherhood. Three years in. Free men's circle every Saturday at 8am — no fee, no application. 800 men in the wider community. A 44-part documentary on the men's mental health crisis. A book on how to start a circle in your own city. One mission: a million men in circles, worldwide. That's the work. That's what we're actually here for."
"BXB is the inner ring. It's what happens when the men who've felt the mission want to go deeper — to build with each other, fund the work, and carry it further than any one of us could alone."

The three things to land — in this order

  • The operating system. "Every man in the room contributes. You don't show up to receive — you show up to build. You run sessions. You host events. You're mentored by the men ahead of you and you mentor the man behind you. That's not a perk. That's how the room actually works."
  • The structure. Pack — small group, accountability, rotates every 90 days. Crew — Build or Scale, depending on where he is in the game. Four weekly calls. In-person two events a week minimum. 60-day onboarding arc that walks him from member to contributor.
  • The filter — load-bearing. "This isn't a room for guys who want to extract value from other people's networks. The men who try that don't last — not because we kick them out, because the room just doesn't work that way. The guys who thrive are the ones who give first and trust that the room gives back."

Tailor it to his specific opening

  • If he said he's isolated → lead with the Pack and the in-person calendar.
  • If he said he's plateaued → lead with the Crew, the strategy calls, and the network effect.
  • If he said he wants impact beyond his business → lead with the mother brand mission and the wider movement.
  • If he said his Bali friendships are surface → lead with the 60-day arc and the contribution model.
  • If he said he doesn't know what's next → lead with the Genius Breakthrough and the 90-day Wealth Mission.
Don't list features. He can read the offer doc for features. Your job is to connect three dots: his problem, the operating system of the room, and the men who'd be beside him.
VII.

Offer and guarantee

10 min
BEAT 07
Price, tier, and the decision
≈ 10 min

Present Club as the standard offer

  • "There are three tiers. The one most men start with is Club — that's three thousand a year. You get the full room: Pack, Crew, all four weekly calls, all the in-person, the 60-day arc, curated introductions. Everything we just talked about."
  • State the price flatly. Don't apologise. Don't justify.

Mention Boardroom as the anchor — most men don't need it

"Above that is Boardroom — seven and a half thousand a year. That's for the guys who know they need quarterly 1:1s with me and Ali, weekly check-ins with Matt on accountability, and a private channel for direct access. Most men don't need that to start. Some do. We'd talk about it if you thought it fit."

Naming Boardroom anchors the value of Club. You're not selling him up unless he's a genuine fit — and most won't be. The mention does the work.

Skip Advisory unless he asks

  • If he asks about a higher tier, mention Advisory ($24K, invitation only after 6 months). Don't volunteer it — it's not for new members.

The hand-back — adapted from a webinar closer that works

"I'm not asking you to decide yes or no right now. I'm asking you to make a fully informed decision. The only way you make a fully informed decision is from the inside, not the outside — the same reason you don't buy a house without walking through it. You get in. You see if everything we said on this call is real. If it is, you stay. If it isn't, the guarantee covers you."

Then the direct ask

"So — does this sound like you, or doesn't it?"
Don't soften the ask. "Do you want to think about it?" is the worst question you can ask. You've spent 45 minutes earning the right to a direct answer. Take it.
G.

How to frame the guarantee

critical
The guarantee is conditional — tied to activation and participation. Do not present it apologetically. It is not a safety net. It is a filter, and it is congruent with everything else you just told him about the room.

The script — adapt, don't memorise

"Here's how the guarantee works — and I want to be upfront, because it's different from what you're used to. Most guarantees protect you from a bad product. Ours protects you from yourself.

Show up for your first 90 days. Fully. Engage with your Pack. Complete your commitments. Attend the calls. Host your event. Mentor a brother. Do the work you'd be doing anyway if you were the man you said you wanted to become.

If after 90 days of full participation you don't feel like a fundamentally different operator — with a real inner circle, a clear mission, and the kind of accountability that actually changes behaviour — you get your money back. Every cent.

The only way this doesn't work is if you don't show up. And I'll tell you now — if you're not willing to show up, don't join. I'd rather you save your money and we'd rather hold the seat for someone who will."

Why this works

It flips the risk frame. An unconditional guarantee says "trust us." A conditional guarantee says "we trust the work." The conditional version is more confident, not less.

It pre-qualifies the member. If he balks at the conditions, he was going to be a passive member anyway. The guarantee filters the wrong men before they're in the room — saves you, saves him, saves the culture.

It's congruent with the Creed. You just told him the operating system is contribution. The guarantee mirrors that. An unconditional refund would undermine the culture you just sold him.

What you're specifically guaranteeing — be concrete

  • Attended at least 4 of the weekly calls in his first 30 days
  • Completed his Genius Breakthrough and posted his Ask
  • Hosted or co-hosted at least one event or session
  • Showed up for his Pack weekly

Confirm with the team what the final activation checklist actually is. These are the likely items — lock them down before this script goes live.

O.

Objection bank

7 objections
Each objection has three parts: the tell (what it's really about), the reframe (how you change the lens), and the script (what you actually say). Pull what you need. Don't read these verbatim — land the reframe in your own words.
O1
"It's a lot of money."
Core · Value
The tell

He hasn't seen the value yet. Price is almost never the real objection — it's the shorthand for "I'm not convinced this gets me where I want to go."

The reframe

Shift from cost to opportunity cost. The year you'd spend figuring it out alone vs. the few months it takes with the right men around you. Re-anchor to what staying stuck for another year actually costs him.

Script — use what fits
"Totally fair. Let me ask something different — what's another year of where you are right now actually costing you? Not in dollars. In the stuff you said you wanted at the start of this call."
"Three thousand for the year. It's a few villa months, or a long weekend somewhere nice. The question isn't whether it's a lot of money. It's whether the next chapter you said you wanted is worth that. Only you can answer that."
"If it was half the price, would you be in? … No? Okay. So it's not the price. It's something else. Let's talk about the something else."
Watch forIf he never brings it back to value after the reframe, price was the cover for something else — usually fear of not following through. Go to O4 or O2.
O2
"I don't have the time."
Core · Fear of failure
The tell

Time is rarely about the calendar. It's usually: "I'm scared of committing to another thing I won't follow through on." He's been burned before — by a course, a coach, a program he didn't finish.

The reframe

Acknowledge the fear without naming it. Then reframe BXB as the thing that makes the other commitments actually happen — the accountability layer that stops the drift.

Script — use what fits
"I hear that. Let me ask though — is it actually time, or is it that you've signed up for things before and not followed through, and you don't want to do that again?"
"The reason this works is you don't have to motivate yourself. Your Pack knows your word. Your accountability lead checks in weekly. If you miss a call, you'll hear about it. Most guys say they got time back — because the stuff they were spinning on for months got solved in a single hotseat."
"Four calls a week, plus two in-person. All of them optional except your Pack. If three or four hours a week is the line between staying where you are and the next version of you — that's a trade I'd take every time."
Watch forIf he softens and says "yeah, that's happened before" — that's gold. Go deeper. The conditional guarantee is built directly for this objection.
O3
"I need to talk to my wife / partner."
Core · Internal sell
The tell

He hasn't sold himself yet. If he had, he wouldn't need to sell her. The spouse is real — but it's also the easiest stall there is.

The reframe

Respect the relationship. Then expose the stall. Help him figure out what he'd need to tell her — which is really what he'd need to tell himself.

Script — use what fits
"Of course, that's a real conversation. What are you going to tell her? … Okay — is that the truth? Because if you're not sold yet, she's not going to be either. Let's get you there first."
"The best partnerships I know — the guy comes home having already made the decision and shares the reasoning. The worst ones — he brings home a question. Which one are you bringing home?"
"If she said yes tomorrow, are you in? … Yes? Great. Then the question isn't really about her. It's about you. What are you still working out?"
Watch forGenuine partnership objections look different — "we have a rule about purchases over X" is real. Don't push through that. Respect it, schedule the follow-up, and hold the seat for 72 hours.
O4
"Let me think about it."
Core · Avoidance
The tell

He's avoiding the decision. The Lone Wolf's default move is to go away and "process" — which usually means the feeling fades and nothing happens. Your job is to respect his need to decide without letting the moment dissolve.

The reframe

Hand the decision back directly. You're not closing — you're asking what's actually in the way. That usually pulls the real objection up to the surface.

Script — use what fits
"Totally fair. When you say think — what specifically are you thinking about? Because if it's something I can answer now, let's just answer it now."
"I'm not asking you to decide yes or no today. I'm asking you to make a fully informed decision — and you can't do that from out here. You can only do it from the inside. The guarantee is built for exactly this."
"Here's what I've noticed. Guys who say 'let me think' and go away — almost none of them come back. Not because the offer got worse. Because the feeling faded. Whatever you felt on this call is the real thing. The longer you sit with it, the quieter it gets."
Watch forIf he names a specific thing he needs to think about — solve it live. If he can't name anything, it's avoidance. Use the guarantee framing and the third script.
O5
"Is this just another mastermind?"
BXB-specific · Category
The tell

He's been burned. Probably paid serious money for a room where he was the audience and the coach was the star. He wants to know you're different — not that you claim to be, but that you structurally are.

The reframe

Don't defend. Differentiate. The BXB operating system is structurally different — and the structure is what makes it work.

Script — use what fits
"Fair question. Most masterminds are a coach in the middle and members around the edge. Everyone pays the coach. The coach is the star. When the coach burns out or gets bored, the room dies. BXB is the opposite — the men in the room are the product. Ryan, Matt, Ali, Julian, Mitch, Alex — we're here, but we're not the centre. The centre is what the men build between themselves."
"The test is simple. In a mastermind, what happens if the coach leaves? The room collapses. In BXB, what happens if any one of us leaves? The room keeps running. That's the difference."
"Every member contributes. You host. You mentor. You run sessions. That's not a perk — that's how the room stays alive. If you're looking for a guru and a mic to pass, you'll be disappointed."
Watch forIf he's still skeptical, ask what specifically burned him last time. Use his words to point to the structural difference. Don't generalise.
O6
"I'm not in Bali full-time — is this for me?"
BXB-specific · Geography
The tell

He's worried he'll be a second-class member. Or that he'll pay for things he can't actually use.

The reframe

BXB runs on calls plus in-person. The calls are the engine; the in-person compounds the value but isn't the condition.

Script — use what fits
"Honestly — part-time guys often get more out of it. They come to Bali three or four times a year with intent, land hard, meet the brotherhood in person, then take the relationships home. We've got members who fly in for events, host trips, and run their remote Pack calls from wherever they are."
"Four calls a week. Bali timezone, but spread across the day. Our satellite circles in Europe and Asia join in regularly. The in-person is the bonus — it's not the thing that decides whether the room works for you."
"If you're in Bali six to eight weeks a year with intent, you'll get what you came for. If you're here two weekends a year with no plan, any room will feel thin. That's on you, not on geography."
Watch forIf he's truly in Bali less than once a year, be honest — BXB works best for men with meaningful Bali time. Don't sell him something he won't use. The long-term cost of a disengaged member is higher than the short-term revenue.
O7
"I already have a coach."
BXB-specific · Overlap
The tell

He thinks BXB and his coach are the same category. Or he's loyal to his coach and doesn't want to feel like he's cheating. Or he's had a bad coaching experience and is lumping you in.

The reframe

Coach and brotherhood are different tools. A coach gives you someone smarter than you telling you what to do. Brotherhood gives you peers who see you, hold you, and build with you. You don't trade one for the other — they do different jobs.

Script — use what fits
"That's great — a good coach is worth keeping. The question is different though. A coach is one person, smarter than you, telling you what to do. Brotherhood is twelve peers who see you, hold you, and build with you. Different tool. Most of our best members have a coach or two. The room doesn't replace the coach — it replaces the isolation."
"What does your coach do well? … And what doesn't your coach give you? … Right — that's what the room is for. Not instead of. Alongside."
"The Lone Wolf pattern is 'one person at a time, one-on-one.' Even if the one person is a coach, you're still alone in the work. BXB is the shift from one-on-one to peer-on-peer. It's a different category entirely."
Watch forIf he's defensive about his coach, back off. Loyalty is a feature, not a bug. Position BXB as the missing layer, not the replacement.
C.

Closing moves

the ask bank
Pull the one that fits. Don't layer them — one clean ask is stronger than three stacked. Pick the one that matches where he is.
The Direct
"So — does this sound like you, or doesn't it?"
The Default Tier
"Club's the right starting point for you. You want to do the full payment for the year, or split it across two or three?"
The Hand-Back
"I'm not asking you to decide from outside. I'm asking you to decide to look inside. The guarantee is built for exactly that — get in, see if everything we said is real. If it is, you stay. If it's not, you're out, no hard feelings."
The Bar-Callback
"At the start of this you said the thing that would need to be true is [X]. Is [X] actually what we've been describing for the last forty minutes? … Then let's do this."
The Wolf-Callback
"You can keep doing this alone and see where another year of it lands you. Or you can do it with the men who've been where you're going. Which man do you want to be a year from now?"
The Long-View
"Picture the men who'll be at your sixtieth. Are they the ones you've got around you now, or are they the ones you haven't met yet? This is how you meet them."
The Seat
"I think this is your seat. But I'll be honest — if you don't want it, I've got other conversations this week and I'd rather hold it for someone who's going to show up. Is it yours, or not?"
The 72-Hour Hold
"If you genuinely need to talk to [wife / business partner], I'll hold the seat for 72 hours. After that it opens back up. Does Friday work for the follow-up?"

Closing anti-patterns — don't do these

  • "Do you want to think about it?" — never offer this. You've earned a decision.
  • "What questions do you have?" — invites the stall. Ask "What's in the way of a yes?" instead.
  • Stacking closes — "It's Boardroom or Club, plus payment plans, plus the guarantee, plus..." One ask. Silence. Let him decide.
  • Discounting — never drop price. If price is the issue, step down a tier. The number holds or the culture doesn't.
  • Convincing after no. If he says no, ask: "Fair. What would have to change?" Then respect the answer.
